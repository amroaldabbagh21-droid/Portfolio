How it all started...

Once upon a time well, in the late 1960s computers were giant machines that couldn’t talk to each other. Then a group of clever researchers working for the U.S. Department of Defense created ARPANET, a project that let a few computers share data through phone lines. It was basically the Internet’s great-great-grandparent.

Fast-forward to the 1980s: scientists connected more networks together and called it “the network of networks.” Then in 1989, a British guy named Tim Berners-Lee invented the World Wide Web, giving us websites, hyperlinks, and the ability to waste hours watching cat videos.

So from four clunky computers to billions of devices the Internet grew into the digital playground we all live in today.

Where we at today...

Today, we live in a hyper-connected world, shaped by inventions born from the internet. Our daily lives are now built on cloud computing, AI assistants, and instant mobile communication. We work remotely, are entertained by on-demand streaming, and socialize through global platforms.

Emerging right now are technologies taking this further: the metaverse for immersive experiences, Web3 and decentralized finance, and a future being built by AI—all direct results of the internet's foundational power.

The AI Revolution: modern Artificial Intelligence, particularly the generative AI and advanced machine learning we see today, is entirely dependent on the internet.

Streaming & On-Demand: The internet broke the tyranny of schedules, revolutionizing entertainment (Spotify, Netflix), education (YouTube, Coursera), and work (Zoom, Slack).

A bit technical...

We often picture the internet as a mystical, invisible force—a silent, wireless whisper that connects us all. We call it "the cloud," a name that perfectly captures this sense of something intangible and everywhere. But this is a beautiful illusion.

The truth is, the internet is profoundly, ruggedly physical. It is quite literally a global nervous system made of cables. When you send an email across an ocean or stream a movie from another continent, your data doesn't magically float through the air. It embarks on an epic, lightspeed journey along a pathway of glass fibers no thicker than a human hair, bundled into cables that lie on the most hostile and remote landscape on Earth: the deep ocean floor.

These submarine cables are the unsung heroes of our connected age. They are engineering marvels, armored in steel and buried in trenches where they cross shallow shores, then laid delicately across the abyssal plains miles beneath the surface. Sharks occasionally nibble on them, and ships' anchors can snag them, but for the most part, they sit in the eternal dark, silently pulsing with light, carrying over 99% of all international data.

So, the next time you video-call a friend on the other side of the world or watch a live event from a distant country, remember the incredible voyage your data is taking. That instant connection travels through a hidden, underwater world of cables—the solid, tangible foundation that makes the magic of the cloud possible. The internet isn't in the air; it's anchored to the Earth itself.
